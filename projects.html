CS 29/PSYC 15 (Impact Design): Personalized Jean Jacket

October-November 2018

This class matched Dartmouth students with an autistic student at a local school for autistic children. The goal of the class was for every group to build a long-lasting and delightful product for their autistic student.

My group was matched with Abby, a 12 year old girl with a developmental age of 4-6 years. Through our visits with Abby, we found out important details such as that she is nonverbal, very social, loves all-things girly & Disney, and has three sisters & doesn’t get as much personalized attention at home. We were tasked with the question of creating a meaningful product and experience for Abby that would be special for her and that she could share with people in her life.

Our final product was a personalized jean jacket. We obtained patches that were customized to Abby's interests (Disney, food, princessess, etc.) and attached velcro to the back of each patch. We also attached velcro to the back and sleeves of the jacket. Our goal was that the jacket would be a long-lasting product, but at the same time, Abby could customize the jacket herself by placing whichever velcro patches on the jacket wherever she wanted.

We used the design thinking process throughout the term by ideating, prototyping, iterating, and finally executing. Abby loved the jacket and got emotional when she received it. She was excited to come to school to wear the jacket and wore it consistently following our final visit.

ENGS 31 (Digital Electronics): Combinational Lock

August 2018

For our final project, my parter and I created a combination lock with LED indicators and an autolock-out mode. Our project required a Basys3 FPGA board, Pmod-Keypad, Pmod-breadboard, 2 LEDs, and 2 resistors. We used VHDL to program the Basys3 board.

Our combinational lock compares a user's 4-digit keypad input to a hardwired solution. The Basys3 board is connected to the breadboard with 2 output LED indicators that are wired to turn on and off depending on the solution. If the user makes three consecutive failed passcode attempts, the autolock feature engages and the lock cannot be operated until ten seconds have passed (in a real world application this would be longer). When the lock is in autolock mode, the characters ‘dEAd’ show up on the seven segment display. This function is similar to that found on many popular smartphones and prevents brute force entry to the device through repeated password attempts.
